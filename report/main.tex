%% Abstract.
\abstract{%
A price chart shows that Bitcoin moved.  It cannot show when, what kind of day, or why.  We present a coordinated multi-scale dashboard that answers all three across one interaction
loop: a Macro long-range overview, a Meso regime-discovery view built on a UMAP projection of an eight-dimensional daily feature vector, and a Micro day-explanation view that pairs intraday price action with same-day GDELT headlines and Polymarket prediction-market odds.  The three views share a single client store, so a brush in one filters the others.  Beyond the chart inventory, the system makes a small but practical data-design contribution: per-window curated GDELT queries and Polymarket event slugs that turn two recency-biased public APIs into a date-aware historical context engine for four case-study windows (COVID 2020, War 2022, Election 2024, Iran 2026).  We validate the workflow on 2026-03-23, when Iran-related tensions coincided with a midday Bitcoin breakout, and trace the narrative end-to-end from macro anomaly to headline timing and prediction-market shifts. A free copy of this paper, all source code, and the cached data artifacts are available at
\url{https://github.com/CZA1006/BTC-Multi-Scale-Visualization}.%
}

\firstsection{Introduction}

\maketitle

Bitcoin (BTC) sits between an asset and a narrative.  Its price has
tripled and halved multiple times since 2019, with each regime change
tied to a different external story: a pandemic crash, a Federal
Reserve pivot, a sovereign war, two US presidential elections, and a
string of geopolitical shocks.  A line chart can show the magnitude
of those moves; it cannot tell the analyst when a regime
began, what kind of trading day sits inside that regime, or
why the market interpreted a given news bundle the way it did.
The three questions are not independent: each requires a different
scale of detail, and the analyst's mental model is built only
when all three are answered consistently for the same date.

This project builds a web-based visual analytics system over a
2{,}677-day BTC record (2019-01-01 to 2026-04-30) that answers all
three questions in a single interaction loop.  The system ingests four heterogeneous data layers, including BTC daily and hourly OHLCV from Yahoo Finance, three external assets (COIN, MSTR, QQQ) on the same calendar, $\sim 80$  thousand GDELT~\cite{gdelt} headlines covering the four case-study windows, and $\sim 6{,}700$ daily price points from 70 curated Polymarket~\cite{polymarket} prediction markets. It then projects each trading day into an eight-dimensional engineered feature vector, consisting of return, open-close change, high-low range, 30-day volume z-score, 7-day and 30-day rolling realised volatility, 30-day drawdown, and an aggregated GDELT news-tone field. This feature vector is the backbone of every cross-view computation in the system and the input to the UMAP embedding and KMeans clustering used in the Meso view.

The dashboard is organised around four analytical tasks distilled
from the project requirements:

\begin{itemize}
  \item[\textbf{T1}] \emph{Market evolution.}~How does BTC evolve over
    the seven-year horizon?
  \item[\textbf{T2}] \emph{Pattern discovery.}~Which trading days form
    typical regimes, and which are abnormal?
  \item[\textbf{T3}] \emph{Day-level explanation.}~For a chosen day,
    what happened hour by hour?
  \item[\textbf{T4}] \emph{Cross-market context.}~How do news flow,
    prediction markets, and external assets relate to BTC?
\end{itemize}

Each task is served by one of three coordinated views (Macro, Meso,
Micro) plus a context layer that overlays GDELT headlines, Polymarket
prediction-market odds, and three external assets (COIN, MSTR, QQQ).
The three views share a single client-side selection store: brushing a
Macro range filters the Meso scatterplot, clicking a Meso point sets the
Micro selected date, and selecting a date in Micro highlights it back in
Macro and Meso.  All four data layers feed the same daily-bucket index,
making cross-view linking a constant-time lookup.

The system was deliberately scoped narrow and deep: rather than
build a broad terminal-style toolkit, we focus on three high-bandwidth
views and a case-study navigator that walks the user through four
prepared windows.  The case study below focuses on 2026-03-23, a date within the Iran tension window, and exercises every link in the chain.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Our design rests on the foundational visualization literature.
Munzner's what/why/how triple~\cite{munzner2014},
Shneiderman's overview-first mantra~\cite{shneiderman1996}, Mackinlay's
channel rankings~\cite{mackinlay1986} and Cleveland and McGill's
perceptual experiments~\cite{cleveland1984} jointly justify our
encoding choices: position-on-common-scale for OHLC and volume,
color-on-diverging-scale only for centred quantities, and a
Macro$\to$Meso$\to$Micro flow that always shows the overview first.
Van Wijk and van Selow's calendar-cell layout~\cite{vanwijk1999} is the
canonical grid encoding we adopt for daily returns; Heer et
al.~\cite{heer2009} characterise the horizon-graph alternative we
considered but did not adopt once event markers were added.  For
multidimensional days we apply UMAP~\cite{mcinnes2018umap} together
with Inselberg's parallel coordinates~\cite{inselberg1985}, and Tufte's
small-multiples and data-ink principles~\cite{tufte2001} drive our
equity-curve sparklines.  The case-study navigator borrows Segel and
Heer's author-driven $\to$ reader-driven
spectrum~\cite{segel2010narrative}; all categorical encodings follow
Wong's color-blindness guidance~\cite{wong2011points} and ColorBrewer
discipline~\cite{colorbrewer}.

Most public crypto dashboards (Glassnode, CoinGecko, TradingView) treat
news as a sidebar decoupled from the chart.  GDELT~\cite{gdelt} and Polymarket~\cite{polymarket} are structurally biased toward today. Our date-aware historical extension to these two APIs is the practical contribution that distinguishes this work from a chart catalog.

\section{Design Tasks and Requirements}
\label{sec:design}

We follow Munzner's nested-model task abstraction~\cite{munzner2014} by mapping each domain question to an abstract task and then to a visualization idiom.  
T1 (\emph{market evolution}) summarizes temporal trends and identifies abnormal days in ordered time-series data, supported by linear and grid time-series layouts. 
T2 (\emph{pattern discovery}) compares multidimensional daily features and reveals recurring market states through dimension reduction, clustering, and parallel coordinates.  
T3 (\emph{day-level explanation}) inspects an aligned multivariate intraday slice using coordinated linear layouts on a common \(x\)-axis.
T4 (\emph{cross-market context}) looks up and compares heterogeneous evidence across external asset prices, news themes, and prediction-market probabilities in a coordinated contextual layout.

Six concrete requirements followed.
\textbf{R1, single-screen overview:} the Macro, Meso, and Micro views should fit on a 1440-px laptop screen without scrolling, which motivates compact encodings such as sparklines where appropriate. 
\textbf{R2, regime visibility:} the Meso view should make the cluster membership of every day in the brushed window legible at a glance through UMAP, cluster hulls, and parallel coordinates. 
\textbf{R3, date-aware context:} the news and prediction-market panels should reflect the selected date rather than the current API state, motivating the curated-bucket data design in \cref{sec:curated}. 
\textbf{R4, cross-scale consistency:} selections should propagate across all views within the same render cycle, allowing users to check a hypothesis across macro, meso, and micro scales without losing context. 
\textbf{R5, accessible encoding:} every diverging scale uses ColorBrewer RdYlGn-7~\cite{colorbrewer}; every categorical scale uses Set2 with BTC orange excluded; chip glyphs use redundant shape and hue encoding~\cite{wong2011points}.  . 
\textbf{R6, lightweight deployment:} the interface should remain small enough for responsive web use, with a target budget of \(\le 6\)~KB gzipped CSS and \(\le 130\)~KB gzipped initial JavaScript. 
The production build meets this budget with 5.00~KB CSS and 106.5~KB JavaScript.

\section{System and Visualization Design}
\label{sec:system}

The system uses a FastAPI backend with three JSON endpoints:
\texttt{/api/overview}, \texttt{/api/meso}, and \texttt{/api/day-detail}.
The frontend is implemented in React 18 with Vite and Zustand for state management.
Visualizations are built with D3 v7 and ECharts v6; no chart library is used as a black box for our novel encodings.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%4.1
\subsection{Macro view (T1): Linear and grid layouts for time series}

The Macro view answers \emph{when}.  Following the standard
temporal-data taxonomy~\cite{munzner2014}, we combine two
complementary layouts: a \emph{linear} layout (the BTC daily timeline,
$x$ = time, $y$ = close price) and a \emph{grid} layout (the calendar
heatmap, one cell per trading day, color-encoding daily return).  We
deliberately avoided the \emph{circular} layout because BTC has no
strong intra-week or intra-month cyclicality at the daily granularity,
and we considered but did not adopt the horizon-graph
alternative~\cite{heer2009} once the event-marker layer was added: the
markers already supply the vertical-density payoff that horizon
graphs provide.

The line chart uses position on a common scale, Mackinlay's highest-ranked channel for quantitative data~\cite{mackinlay1986}. We overlay five canonical event markers, including FOMC pivots, war onsets, and ETF approvals, using shape together with hue to distinguish event categories. 
The calendar heatmap uses a ColorBrewer RdYlGn-7 diverging scale~\cite{colorbrewer} anchored at zero, with green indicating positive return, red indicating negative return, and yellow indicating near-zero return. To reduce reliance on color alone, a small dot in the corner of each cell encodes the GDELT event-density signal. Clicking a cell updates \texttt{selectedDate}, while brushing the timeline updates \texttt{selectedTimeRange} (\cref{fig:macro}). Both selections are propagated through the shared store to support coordinated brushing and linking across views.


%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%4.2 meso

\subsection{Meso view (T2): Multidimensional analysis}

The Meso view answers \emph{what kind of day}.  We combine dimension reduction and clustering to reveal recurring patterns in the eight-dimensional daily feature space. 
We project each daily feature vector to 2D using UMAP~\cite{mcinnes2018umap}, which is suitable for capturing nonlinear neighborhood structure in exploratory analysis. We then cluster the original feature vectors with KMeans and overlay \(k=5\) convex hulls on the projection.
Spatial proximity in the scatter encodes market similarity: nearby points are similar trading days; cluster color encodes the regime label.  
We use the ColorBrewer Set2 categorical palette and exclude orange to avoid ambiguity with the primary BTC encoding, following the principle that color should primarily support labeling rather than decoration~\cite{tufte2001}.



A regime summary band below the scatter makes the cluster story
quantitative through three coordinated panels.  
~(1)~A cluster summary table reports $n$, mean daily return, annualized volatility,and a 60-point equity-curve \emph{sparkline} for each
cluster~\cite{tufte2001}. These sparklines act as small multiples, allowing users to compare regime shapes without leaving the overview.  
~(2)~A toggleable parallel-coordinates plot~\cite{inselberg1985} over the seven engineered features restores the axes UMAP collapsed; the selected day is drawn as a thick line on top of a faded cluster average, supporting per-day-vs-regime
comparison directly.
~(3)~A $4{\times}4$ cross-asset correlation matrix (Pearson over the active window) over BTC, COIN, MSTR, and QQQ uses a green$\leftrightarrow$red diverging scale anchored at zero.
Selecting a cluster in the legend filters all three panels in one
render (\cref{fig:meso}).

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%4.3
\subsection{Micro view (T3, T4): Intraday detail and context layer}

The Micro view answers \emph{what happened} on a selected day and \emph{how} cross-market signals relate to it.  The first two elements support T3 by showing what happened within the day, while the remaining three support T4 by adding external assets, news narratives, and prediction-market context.
~(1)Summary cards report selected-day open/high/low/close, intraday range, and 24-hour change; the up/down chip uses redundant shape (\,$\blacktriangle/\blacktriangledown$\,) and hue, satisfying the redundancy guideline for color-blind accessibility~\cite{wong2011points}.
~(2)~The intraday chart follows the time-series linear-layout idiom: two stacked grids (candlesticks above, volume bars below) sharing one $x$-axis, so position-on-common-scale carries the alignment between price and traded volume (Mackinlay's top-ranked channel~\cite{mackinlay1986}; \cref{fig:micro-intraday}).
~(3)~The external-asset context strip shows three sparklines for COIN, MSTR, and QQQ over the same window, allowing users to compare whether related assets moved consistently with BTC. 
~(4)~The headline panel lists GDELT articles for the selected day, sorted by tone and tagged with one of seven theme categories: war, election, covid, regulation, macro, crypto, and other. This design uses categorical tags rather than word clouds, keeping attention on the dominant narrative context instead of isolated keywords. The active GDELT bucket, corresponding to the curated case-study window, is shown as a provenance line above the panel. 
~(5)~The Polymarket panel renders per-curated-market cards, each showing a daily YES-probability sparkline with a marker at the
selected date, the YES price observed at that date, and the current
resolution price (\cref{fig:micro-context}).


%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%4.4
\subsection{Date-aware historical context: the data-design contribution}
\label{sec:curated}

The two public APIs used for T4 are strongly recency-biased in practice. 
A direct GDELT DOC query using \texttt{bitcoin OR crypto} tended to return crypto-centered headlines, leaving COVID-2020 and election-2024 days with sparse contextual evidence. 
Similarly, Polymarket keyword search primarily surfaces current or active markets, which is insufficient for asking what prediction markets implied on a historical date such as 2024-11-05.

We address this limitation with a curated-bucket pattern. 
Each of the four case-study windows (COVID, War, Election, Iran) owns:

\begin{itemize}
  \item A tailored GDELT DOC query (e.g., the 2022 War bucket queries
    \texttt{(ukraine OR russia OR putin OR sanctions OR swift OR \ldots)})
    that keeps a \texttt{bitcoin OR crypto} tail clause so the asset
    chart is always anchored, plus a six-category theme map (war,
    election, covid, regulation, macro, crypto) so non-crypto windows
    still produce a populated theme-tagged headline panel.
  \item A curated list of Polymarket event slugs covering the corresponding historical event window, such as the 2024 presidential election and the 2025 U.S.--Iran nuclear-deal market. 
  For each slug we fetch the full Gamma event payload and the per-market CLOB \texttt{/prices-history} daily series, then filter to markets active on the selected date with a 14-day post-resolution grace window so a market that resolved the day before still surfaces with its closing probability.  A \$1k volume floor and top-8 cap drop the long tail of zero-volume markets that would otherwise swamp the panel.
\end{itemize}

A bulk-warm script seeds 287 cached headline payloads and 6{,}708
Polymarket price points across the four windows.  An auto-rollup step
re-emits \texttt{gdelt\_daily\_signals.csv} after every fetch so the
Macro heatmap dot indicator stays in sync.  
This data-design layer converts recency-biased API outputs into a date-aware historical context engine, enabling the system to reconstruct what news and prediction-market signals were available around the selected trading day. 
Beyond the visual arrangement of charts, this curated historical retrieval pipeline is a central practical contribution of the system.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%4.5
\subsection{Implementation}
\label{sec:impl}

The backend is a FastAPI service of $\sim$1{,}000 lines of Python
exposing three JSON endpoints.  
The frontend is a Vite-based React 18 single-page application of \(\sim\)3{,}500 lines of JavaScript and JSX, with selection state held in a Zustand store of seven keys (\texttt{selectedTimeRange}, \texttt{selectedDate}, \texttt{selectedCluster}, \ldots) that every view subscribes to.  
All custom encodings (calendar heatmap, UMAP scatter with hulls, parallel coordinates, correlation matrix, Polymarket sparkline-with-marker) are written directly in D3~v7 and SVG; the intraday OHLC and volume chart is the only ECharts dependency, code-split out of the main bundle so it only loads when the Micro view becomes visible.  
The production build measures 5.00~KB of CSS and 106.5~KB of JavaScript gzipped, comfortably inside the bundle budget defined in
\cref{sec:design}.

\section{Case Study: 2026-03-23, Iran Tension}
\label{sec:case}

We validate the workflow through a deep case study on 2026-03-23, a trading day within the curated Iran-tension window. The analysis starts from the case-study navigator, assuming that the user has no prior knowledge of this specific date.

\textbf{T1: Locate the anomaly.} 
The user selects the Iran Tension chip, and the Macro window updates to 2026-03-01\,\(\to\)\,2026-04-30. In the calendar heatmap (\cref{fig:macro}), 2026-03-23 appears as a deep green cell, indicating a strong positive-return day within an otherwise mixed window. Hovering reveals the daily return, while clicking the cell sets \texttt{selectedDate} and propagates the selection to the Meso and Micro views.

\textbf{T2: Characterize the regime.} 
In the Meso view (\cref{fig:meso}), the selected day falls inside the Bullish-Momentum cluster, which also contains other midday-breakout days from the 2024 election cycle. The parallel-coordinates overlay shows that the profile of 2026-03-23 is above the cluster mean on \texttt{rolling\_volatility\_7d} and well below the mean on \texttt{drawdown\_from\_30d\_high}. This combination suggests a volatile upward move rather than a correction from a recent peak. The cluster summary table further shows that this regime has the steepest equity-curve sparkline among the populated clusters in the active window.

\textbf{T3: Drill into the day.}  In the Micro view's intraday chart
(\cref{fig:micro-intraday}), 2026-03-23 splits cleanly into three
regimes: a flat-to-weak morning UTC session, a sharp midday breakout
(the single bar in which BTC clears the prior day's high on visibly
elevated volume), and a sustained afternoon rally.  The summary cards
report the day's intraday range and 24-hour change with a green
$\blacktriangle$ chip.

\textbf{T4: Add contextual evidence.}  
The external-asset strip shows that COIN and MSTR rallied in step with BTC, suggesting that the move was aligned with related crypto-equity assets rather than isolated to BTC alone. 
The headline panel (\cref{fig:micro-context}) provides the corresponding news chronology. Morning headlines are mainly negative and geopolitical, including stalled Iran-deal talks and regional tension. Around the breakout, mainstream financial outlets begin reporting that BTC had broken above a key level, indicating that the market moves first and the press reports second. Afternoon headlines then shift toward mixed political and regulatory commentary. 
The curated Polymarket cards show small but visible YES-probability shifts in the U.S.--Iran nuclear-deal and war-related markets, with 2026-03-26 marker placed near the local inflection in the sparkline.

\textbf{Case-study finding.}  
No single panel proves causality; the flow provides the evidence.  
Macro localises the anomaly, Meso shows that anomalies of this type cluster with prior breakouts, Micro shows the intraday regime change, and the headline and Polymarket layer supplies a coherent narrative for why the regime change happened when it did.  
This story depends on the date-aware data layer. Without it, the headline panel would have shown bitcoin-only news, the Polymarket cards would have shown today's markets, and the Meso cluster would have lacked the historical comparators that put a 2026 breakout in the same neighbourhood as 2024 election-night and 2020 post-halving days.  The system's contribution is the coherence of the loop, not any one chart.

A concrete finding becomes visible only through this multi-scale alignment.  
Because the headline panel is timestamped at 15-minute granularity and the intraday chart uses the same UTC \(x\)-axis, the user can see that on 2026-03-23 BTC breakout precedes the news-volume peak.
The volume spike therefore precedes much of the breakout coverage, rather than following it.  
This micro-scale lead-lag pattern is difficult to observe from a single daily price chart or from an isolated news feed.  
It is only legible because the two views share both a $y$-axis-aligned data backbone (daily UTC) and a coordinated selection.

\subsection{Design contribution and findings}

The system deliberately relies on familiar visualization idioms, including time-series layouts, calendar heatmaps, scatterplots, parallel coordinates, sparklines, and coordinated panels. The design contribution lies in how these views are linked into a coherent multi-scale workflow, rather than in introducing a new chart type. 
The central data-design contribution is the date-aware historical context layer described in \cref{sec:curated}. 
By combining per-window curated GDELT queries with curated Polymarket event slugs, the system converts recency-biased public API outputs into historical contextual evidence for selected trading days. This enables the case study workflow to operate normally on the dates of 2020, 2022 and 2024.
The 2026-03-23 case study (\cref{sec:case}) demonstrates how the coordinated workflow supports interpretation across tasks. 
The Macro view identifies the day as a strong positive-return anomaly, the Meso view relates it to prior breakout-like regimes, the Micro view separates the day into distinct intraday phases, and the contextual panels add external assets, headlines, and prediction-market signals. 
In this case, the key observation is a lead-lag pattern between price movement and news volume. 
BTC begins its midday breakout before related news volume reaches its peak, a pattern that becomes visible only because intraday price, trading volume, and headline volume are aligned on a common UTC \(x\)-axis and coordinated through the same linked selection.
The finding is therefore methodological: the system makes such cross-scale timing relationships observable, while avoiding a claim that any single panel establishes causality.

\subsection{Limitations}

The GDELT cache is approximately \(60\%\)  complete across the COVID,
War, and Election windows (the Iran window is fully covered).  
Because the GDELT DOC API rate-limits requests to one per five seconds, completing the remaining cache is primarily an incremental data-collection step rather than a change to the system design or code. 
Polymarket coverage is by design limited to the 2024 Election and 2026 Iran windows; the 2020 and 2022 windows return a friendly ``no Polymarket coverage for this period'' message rather than empty cards.  
A light-theme palette and a side-by-side cross-window comparison view are left for future work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This work presents a compact visual analytics system for multi-scale Bitcoin analysis. 
Its contribution is not a new chart type, but a coordinated workflow that links temporal overview, regime characterization, intraday inspection, and historical context within one interaction loop. 
A key requirement is date-aware access to news and prediction-market data, so the contextual panels reflect the selected trading day rather than the current API state.
The case study traces the 2026-03-23 Iran-tension breakout from the deepest heatmap cell to the midday volume spike to the headline and Polymarket narrative that explains the regime change, with no panel switch and no manual cross-referencing.

The work leads to three main takeaways.   
(i) Recurring market regimes are visible once daily features are projected and clustered, where a price line alone would obscure them.
 (ii) A single-channel view is insufficient: return, volatility, drawdown, and volume must be co-displayed for anomalies to surface.  
(iii) External signals (news, prediction markets) are best read as supporting evidence, not direct proof of causality; the dashboard is designed to make that distinction visible to the user rather than hide it.
